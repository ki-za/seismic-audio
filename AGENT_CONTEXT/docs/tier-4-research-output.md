# Audio Algorithms Catalog for Production Seismic Sonification

## Executive view

Big picture first. For your pipeline, four things move the needle most: bandlimited resampling, targeted transient repair, frequency-aware dynamics, and proper finishing on loudness plus dither. If those are missing, the audio keeps the brittle, clicky, “cheap fast render” character. Once those are in place, tasteful saturation, mono-safe width, and controlled ambience can make the material feel warmer and more installation-ready without hiding the seismic identity. The research base is strong on these points: polyphase and windowed-sinc resampling are the standard practical answers for high-quality sample-rate conversion; multiband or dynamic EQ beats blunt wideband control when harshness is concentrated in the upper band; LUFS plus true-peak is the accepted loudness framework; and triangular dither is the safest last step when reducing word length. citeturn23view1turn38view1turn23view3turn34view1turn35view0turn35view1turn15view1

My recommended default chain for a **core production mode** is this: source-domain spike repair; bandlimited resampling to the target audio rate; gentle HF-aware control; 3-band dynamics; oversampled asymmetric saturation; true-peak limiting; integrated loudness normalisation; then 16-bit dither on export. Between quiet events, use a downward expander instead of a hard gate, then add a shaped comfort-noise bed if the room feels unnaturally dead. For **artistic modes**, add mono-safe pseudo-stereo and optional tape or wavefold colour after the core chain, never before the repair and resampling stages. That ordering follows the usual logic in restoration, mastering, and resampling research: clean discontinuities early, keep nonlinearity after bandlimiting, and leave loudness and requantisation until the very end. citeturn26view0turn26view1turn29view0turn30view1turn35view1turn39view1turn39view0turn15view1

## Resampling and transient repair

**🔴 P0 Polyphase windowed-sinc FIR resampler** — This is the workhorse answer for your current linear-interpolation export path. **What it solves:** linear interpolation is not considered adequate for high-quality resampling, while polyphase FIR explicitly upsamples, low-pass filters, and downsamples in a way designed for proper bandlimited conversion. In your case, the real enemy is not just “aliasing” in the casual sense; it is image rejection, HF droop, and garbage fed into later nonlinear stages. **Signal-flow position:** first serious processing stage, before saturation, multiband compression, or any loudness work. **Knobs:** cutoff around 0.90–0.96 of destination Nyquist, Kaiser window β roughly 5–10, symmetric odd-length FIR, and precomputed phase tables. A good practical split is ~16–32 taps per phase for a low-latency real-time path and ~64–256 taps per phase for offline export. **Cost:** moderate in polyphase form, expensive in naïve direct sinc form. **Web Audio notes:** not a native node; implement as pure TypeScript in `domain/sonification.ts`, then use from `bridge/recorder.ts` for offline export. For real time, it belongs in an `AudioWorklet` only if browser-side SRC quality must match export quality. **Aesthetic contribution:** less brittle top end, less “needle spray”, less tiring upper grit, cleaner density after compression and saturation. **References/implementations:** Julius Smith’s resampling material, `scipy.signal.resample_poly`, and urllibsampleratehttps://libsndfile.github.io/libsamplerate/ are the right reference points. **Mode:** core. citeturn23view1turn23view0turn38view1turn23view3turn25view2

**🟡 P1 Lanczos resampler** — Lanczos is still windowed sinc, but lighter. **What it solves:** a good middle ground where full polyphase Kaiser feels too heavy. Lanczos is a truncated sinc windowed by another sinc; it can preserve amplitude well near Nyquist with smaller kernels, but it does not perfectly satisfy partition of unity and can ring on hard edges. **Signal-flow position:** same place as the FIR resampler, but best reserved for a fast preview or “draft quality” render, not your final gallery export. **Knobs:** order 3–5 is the usual practical zone; larger kernels sound cleaner but cost more and ring more. **Cost:** cheap to moderate. **Web Audio notes:** suitable for a fast pure-TS preview renderer; if used, keep the full FIR path for export. **Aesthetic contribution:** noticeably better than linear, but not the “forget it’s there” cleanliness of a strong polyphase FIR. **References/implementations:** the Gamma interpolation review and modern Lanczos audio resampler implementations are useful guides. **Mode:** core preview only. citeturn25view0turn25view1

**🔴 P0 Short-window impulse suppressor using median or Hampel logic** — This is your cheapest practical de-clicker. **What it solves:** isolated sample spikes and tiny outliers that become audible pops after time compression. Median filters are robust to impulsive outliers, which is exactly why they have long been used in click-removal systems. **Signal-flow position:** ideally twice: first on the original count stream using a derivative-based outlier detector, and again after resampling with a very conservative micro-window if needed. **Knobs:** replace only events shorter than roughly 0.25–1 ms in the audio domain or 1–2 source samples in the seismic domain; threshold from local median absolute deviation rather than fixed absolute amplitude; very small windows only. **Cost:** cheap. **Web Audio notes:** easy in pure TS or `AudioWorklet`; no native node exists. **Aesthetic contribution:** removes the nasty “digital vinyl crack” layer while preserving true earthquakes better than broad smoothing. **References/implementations:** real-time median filtering for click removal is well documented and explicitly designed for efficient DSP use. **Mode:** core. citeturn26view3turn26view2

**🟡 P1 Offline AR de-click interpolation** — This is the better export-grade repair tool when the click detector is confident. **What it solves:** localised degradations and missing-sample bursts without the dulling that simple smoothing causes. The AR literature treats restoration as detection plus reconstruction, and it is particularly suited to short corrupted regions. **Signal-flow position:** before loudness, before dither, ideally before heavy multiband or saturation so the rest of the chain never sees the defect. **Knobs:** AR order, interpolation span, confidence threshold, and maximum replaced length. Keep it for short events only; it is not a general denoiser. **Cost:** moderate offline, too heavy and finicky for simple browser graph DSP. **Web Audio notes:** export-only in `bridge/recorder.ts`. **Aesthetic contribution:** fewer audible pops with less smearing than brute-force low-pass fixes. **References/implementations:** Esquef; Janssen/Oudre-style AR interpolation; broader audio-restoration surveys. **Mode:** core offline only. citeturn26view0turn26view1turn26view2

**🔴 P0 Look-ahead brickwall limiter with a soft-clip stage before it** — This is the last line of defence against transient spikes. **What it solves:** post-saturation and post-makeup peaks, plus pops that slip through repair. A properly smoothed look-ahead gain envelope is the standard way to stop overshoots without hard clipping every peak; production limiters add adaptive release, true-peak awareness, and often a clipper stage. **Signal-flow position:** last dynamics stage, just before loudness verification and export. **Knobs:** ceiling, look-ahead, release, stereo link, and clipper drive. Practical starts: look-ahead around 0.3–3 ms for tight control or 3–10 ms for gentler action, adaptive release if possible, ceiling around −1 dBTP for safety. **Cost:** cheap to moderate. **Web Audio notes:** `DynamicsCompressorNode` exists, but if you want a real look-ahead limiter with intelligible behaviour, use custom DSP in an `AudioWorklet` or offline render code. **Aesthetic contribution:** less splat, less stabby top, more “finished master” behaviour. **References/implementations:** Signalsmith’s design article and modern look-ahead limiter manuals are good practical references. **Mode:** core. citeturn29view0turn29view2turn17search2

## Tone, density and dynamics

**🟡 P1 Asymmetric soft-knee saturator** — Plain `tanh` is safe but bland. A better core saturator is a controllable asymmetric soft clipper with a knee parameter and variable DC-compensated asymmetry. **What it solves:** excessive sterility after repair and compression, while avoiding the brittle edge of hard clipping. Research on digital soft clipping shows that knee shape matters, and asymmetrical clipping introduces both even and odd harmonics, whereas symmetrical clipping emphasises odd harmonics. **Signal-flow position:** after broad corrective filtering and usually after multiband compression, so it enriches a controlled signal; oversample around the nonlinearity. **Knobs:** drive, knee width, asymmetry amount, wet/dry, and post trim. Start subtle: drive 1.1–2.5×, asymmetry 5–20%, parallel mix 5–30%. **Cost:** cheap; anti-aliased versions are moderate. **Web Audio notes:** `WaveShaperNode` is a native fit and even exposes built-in oversampling, but advanced anti-aliased or stateful curves need custom DSP. **Aesthetic contribution:** warmth, density, and slight perceived loudness without immediately shouting “distortion”. **References/implementations:** soft-clipping analysis, WaveShaperNode, and the alias-suppression literature around nonlinear waveshaping. **Mode:** core. citeturn30view0turn17search1turn17search16turn30view1turn6search18

**🟡 P1 Tape-style saturator** — Use this when you want character, not just clipping. **What it solves:** the source is harsh and narrow, so a tape-like stage can add mild compression, bias-like asymmetry, and some soft HF rounding in one move. Physical modelling research on tape machines exists and is real-time capable, so this is no longer hand-wavy plugin folklore. **Signal-flow position:** after corrective dynamics and before the final limiter; optionally before the widener so the stereo field keeps the coloured tone, not vice versa. **Knobs:** drive, bias/asymmetry, HF loss, hysteresis strength, and optional wow/flutter depth. Keep wow/flutter off in core mode. **Cost:** moderate. **Web Audio notes:** not a native node; implement a reduced tape stage in an `AudioWorklet` or export path. **Aesthetic contribution:** “warmer, denser, less clicky” is exactly where tape models shine when used lightly. **References/implementations:** Chowdhury’s real-time tape model is the cleanest academic anchor here. **Mode:** core if subtle, artistic if pushed. citeturn30view2turn22search3

**🟡 P1 Three-band Linkwitz–Riley multiband compressor** — This is your main upgrade over a single `DynamicsCompressorNode`. **What it solves:** harsh HF noise, mid-band event density, and low-band rumble each want different thresholds and time constants. Multiband compression reduces pumping and intermodulation compared with one wideband compressor, and Linkwitz–Riley remains the standard minimum-phase crossover because the bands sum flat and in phase at crossover. **Signal-flow position:** after basic corrective EQ and before saturation, so saturation receives more even-band energy. **Knobs:** crossover frequencies, per-band threshold, ratio, attack, release, makeup, and link strategy. Good starting bands: roughly low <200–300 Hz, mid 200–300 Hz to 2–3 kHz, high >2–3 kHz. Low band: slower attack, lower ratio. High band: faster attack, a bit more ratio. **Cost:** moderate. **Web Audio notes:** native biquads can build the crossovers, but sample-accurate sidechains and recombination are much cleaner in an `AudioWorklet`. **Aesthetic contribution:** weight without boom, bite without spit, and more stable detail during dense seismic passages. **References/implementations:** Linkwitz–Riley crossover literature and crossover-network reviews. **Mode:** core. citeturn7search1turn40view0turn40view1

**⚪ P3 Multiband waveshaping, Chebyshev harmonic design, and wavefolding** — This is where the fun lives. **What it solves:** richer harmonic design than vanilla clipping, and more control over which bands get excited. Multiband waveshaping literature explicitly targets lower intermodulation and more harmonic, less metallic results. Chebyshev polynomials are the classic way to generate targeted harmonics from sinusoidal components, while wavefolders create a more obviously synthetic, folded timbre. **Signal-flow position:** after a band split; blend in parallel. Keep the dry path intact. **Knobs:** per-band drive, harmonic recipe, fold depth, output mix, and which bands are excluded. For seismic material, exclude the harshest upper band by default and act mostly in the low-mid and mid bands. **Cost:** moderate to expensive, especially if anti-aliased. **Web Audio notes:** `WaveShaperNode` can do the simple version; serious multiband and bandlimited wavefolding belong in an `AudioWorklet` or offline. **Aesthetic contribution:** can make the earth sound larger, stranger, and more “instrumental” without fully losing source identity, but easy to overcook. **References/implementations:** multiband waveshaping distortion research, Aphex-style enhancer architecture, and anti-aliased wavefolder work. **Mode:** artistic. citeturn32view0turn33view3turn33view2turn33view0

## Harshness, ambience and finishing

**🟡 P1 Relative-threshold de-esser or dynamic high-shelf** — This is the single most targeted cure for pitch-shifted instrument noise. **What it solves:** the harsh band is not constant; it flares when the upper spectrum dominates. Good de-essers compare the critical HF band to the rest of the signal, so they act on relative brightness rather than absolute level. That is a very good fit for your material because seismic passages vary wildly in overall loudness. **Signal-flow position:** before final saturation if the source is brutally sharp, or after multiband compression if you want the compressor to feel the full signal first. **Knobs:** detector band or shelf start, relative threshold, attack, release, maximum cut. Practical start: detector around 2–8 kHz, or dynamic shelf beginning around 4–6 kHz; attack in the sub-5 ms region; release around 30–150 ms. **Cost:** cheap in a sidechain-biquad design, moderate in FFT form. **Web Audio notes:** simple sidechain versions need custom DSP; there is no native de-esser node. **Aesthetic contribution:** less spit and glare, more listenable long-form playback, without simply blanketing the whole top end. **References/implementations:** frequency-domain de-essing, dynamic-EQ overview papers, and professional de-esser manuals using relative thresholds and crossover techniques. **Mode:** core. citeturn34view0turn34view1turn34view2turn34view3

**🟡 P1 Dynamic EQ as the adaptive-filter layer** — Think of this as the grown-up version of your static `SoundMode` filters. **What it solves:** input-dependent harshness and narrow resonant bands that do not justify permanent EQ cuts. Dynamic EQ is explicitly described as the closest thing to a general frequency-and-dynamics tool; it can behave like EQ, dynamic EQ, or multiband compression depending on the settings. **Signal-flow position:** after resampling and initial level staging, before saturator and limiter. **Knobs:** centre frequency or shelf start, Q/bandwidth, threshold, attack, release, range. A useful seismic utility band is a gentle dynamic high shelf above 4–8 kHz with at most 1–6 dB reduction. **Cost:** moderate. **Web Audio notes:** an IIR or biquad band with sidechain control is feasible in an `AudioWorklet`. **Aesthetic contribution:** smarter HF control; the sound stays alive when it can, and only ducks when it must. **References/implementations:** dynamic equaliser literature and de-esser designs. **Mode:** core. citeturn34view1turn34view0

**🟡 P1 Downward expander with shaped comfort noise** — Hard gates make galleries feel broken. **What it solves:** the unsettling leap from “something happened” to absolute digital black. A gate is just an extreme expander, and the difficult part is always threshold and timing; comfort-noise standards exist precisely because dead silence feels unnatural in communications systems. **Signal-flow position:** late in the chain, after denser tone work but before final limiting; the comfort-noise bed is added after the expander. **Knobs:** threshold, ratio/depth, hold, release, and comfort-noise level plus colour. Start with a **soft expander**, not a full gate: ratios around 1.2:1–2:1, depth 6–18 dB, hold 50–300 ms, release 200–1000 ms. Shape the added noise toward the long-term quiet-spectrum of the piece, and keep it low enough that it reads as room-bed, not hiss. **Cost:** trivial for the expander, cheap for noise injection. **Web Audio notes:** easy custom DSP; no native comfort-noise node. **Aesthetic contribution:** keeps the room alive and preserves a sense of continuous geologic presence. **References/implementations:** gate/expander literature and RFC comfort-noise guidance. **Mode:** core. citeturn39view1turn39view0

**🟢 P2 Spectral gate with spectral floor and smoothing** — Use this carefully. **What it solves:** broadband hiss beds or band-limited static that a simple expander cannot catch. **Signal-flow position:** offline only, before saturation and loudness stages. **Knobs:** noise estimate, over-subtraction amount, spectral floor, time smoothing, frequency smoothing. **Cost:** moderate to expensive. **Web Audio notes:** offline render or `AudioWorklet` FFT only; not worth the complexity for the first release. **Aesthetic contribution:** can create cleaner negative space, but classic spectral subtraction is notorious for “musical noise” if implemented badly. **References/implementations:** spectral-subtraction literature explicitly warns about metallic, narrowband residual artefacts. **Mode:** polish only, not default. citeturn39view2

**🔴 P0 Integrated loudness plus true-peak normalisation** — Peak normalisation is not enough. **What it solves:** inconsistent perceived level across pieces and playback systems. BS.1770 gives the low-complexity loudness algorithm; EBU R128 makes programme loudness, loudness range, and maximum true peak the operational framework. For a looping gallery piece, the engineering target should be chosen for comfort and dynamic headroom, not streaming bravado. **Signal-flow position:** final offline render pass, after all creative DSP. **Knobs:** integrated LUFS target and true-peak ceiling. My recommendation for installations is **start at about −18 LUFS integrated and −1 dBTP ceiling**, then adjust to room conditions. That is an engineering recommendation, not a broadcast law: AES guidance explicitly notes that targets lower than −16 LUFS, such as −18 LUFS, improve sound quality by allowing a higher peak-to-loudness ratio. If the room is very quiet, −20 LUFS may be better; if it is a trade-show style noisy space, closer to −16 LUFS may be justified. **Cost:** cheap to moderate, but live integrated LUFS needs block history and threshold recomputation. **Web Audio notes:** real-time meter is feasible; a compliant integrated meter uses 400 ms blocks with 75% overlap and a two-stage gate, while EBU momentary and short-term use 0.4 s and 3 s windows. **Aesthetic contribution:** consistent playback comfort and less listener fatigue over long cycles. **References/implementations:** ITU-R BS.1770, EBU R128, EBU Tech 3341, and AES streaming loudness recommendations. **Mode:** core. citeturn35view0turn35view1turn35view2turn35view3turn36view0turn36view2turn37view0turn37view1

**🔴 P0 TPDF dither, with noise-shaped dither as an optional variant** — This should be the last line in export. **What it solves:** correlated quantisation distortion from float-to-int16 truncation. The classic dither literature is blunt here: triangular-PDF dither of 2 LSB peak-to-peak is the unique optimal nonsubtractive choice that removes input-dependent distortion and minimises noise modulation; noise shaping can then spectrally push the error into less audible regions. **Signal-flow position:** the very last step, only once, after loudness render and true-peak verification. **Knobs:** dither type and optional shaper. For archival honesty and predictable downstream behaviour, plain TPDF is the safer default. Low-order psychoacoustic noise shaping is fine for a final “listener” export, but I would not use aggressive shaping on files that may be processed again. **Cost:** trivial for TPDF, cheap for low-order noise shaping. **Web Audio notes:** export-only in `bridge/recorder.ts`. **Aesthetic contribution:** less gritty decay texture and fewer low-level quantisation tones. **References/implementations:** Lipshitz, Vanderkooy, and Wannamaker remain the canonical references. **Mode:** core for TPDF, polish for noise shaping. citeturn14view1turn15view1turn15view2

## Space and artistic motion

**🟡 P1 Downmix-compatible pseudo-stereo filter pair** — This is the safest widener for installations. **What it solves:** mono seismic audio feels flat, but brute-force widening often collapses badly in mono. The pseudo-stereo literature gives a neat constraint: design left and right filters so their sum equals a delayed version of the original input, which preserves downmix compatibility while decorrelating the stereo channels. **Signal-flow position:** late, after tone work, before final limiter. **Knobs:** decorrelation strength, group delay, and the frequency regions where the filters diverge. Keep low frequencies close to mono. **Cost:** moderate. **Web Audio notes:** implement with FIRs or IIR pairs in an `AudioWorklet`; no native node does this for you. **Aesthetic contribution:** width and immersion without the “what happened when we summed it to mono?” disaster. **References/implementations:** the DAFx pseudo-stereo paper is directly on point. **Mode:** core spatial mode. citeturn20view0

**🟢 P2 Frequency-dependent decorrelation widener with transient bypass** — This is the modern “safe widening” option. **What it solves:** you want width in the mids and highs, but you do not want low-end phantom-centre drift or transient chirping. Recent widening work uses decorrelators such as allpass cascades or velvet-noise filters, mixes that against the dry signal, and bypasses the widener on detected onsets to avoid transient smearing. **Signal-flow position:** same as the pseudo-stereo stage, but only in a stereo mode. **Knobs:** low-band width, high-band width, crossover frequency, decorrelator type, transient-bypass sensitivity. For seismic content, hold the bass mostly mono and spread only upper bands. **Cost:** moderate. **Web Audio notes:** `ChannelSplitterNode`/`ChannelMergerNode` help with routing, but the actual decorrelation logic belongs in custom DSP. **Aesthetic contribution:** the room gets bigger; the sound has air and motion without feeling like a gimmick. **References/implementations:** the 2024 open-source widener paper is especially relevant. **Mode:** polish. citeturn20view1turn20view2turn18search1turn18search5

**⚪ P3 Haas delay widening** — Small caves, big risk. **What it solves:** a quick sense of width and spaciousness. **Signal-flow position:** parallel aux only, never as the only stereo strategy. **Knobs:** 2–15 ms for subtle use, low wet level, HF-only send. **Cost:** trivial. **Web Audio notes:** easy with `DelayNode`. **Aesthetic contribution:** instant width. **Why not core:** Haas widens by short delays, and short delays create comb filtering. That is a known sound-reinforcement problem, so this technique is fragile for installations and mono playback. Use it only as an artistic seasoning and only on the upper band. **References/implementations:** Haas practice guides and AES comb-filtering work. **Mode:** artistic. citeturn20view3turn19view2turn17search3

**⚪ P3 Slow wow/flutter, filter drift, and micro-pitch modulation** — Nice spice. Not dinner. **What it solves:** static long-form loops can feel too literal; very slow modulation can make them breathe. Wow and flutter are literally slow and fast speed variations that frequency-modulate the signal, and tape models expose them as controllable character parameters. **Signal-flow position:** after the main saturator or inside a tape-style artistic module. **Knobs:** wow rate roughly sub-1 Hz, flutter a few Hz, very low depth; for filter drift, tiny shelf or cutoff movement at glacial rates. Keep modulation identical in both channels unless you are intentionally working only in a side branch. **Cost:** cheap to moderate. **Web Audio notes:** can be driven by `DelayNode` modulation or within a custom tape module. **Aesthetic contribution:** subtle motion and analogue feel. **Mode:** artistic only. citeturn22search0turn30view2turn22search7

## Codebase fit and rollout

For your architecture, the clean split is straightforward. Put **pure kernels and coefficient generators** in `domain/sonification.ts`: polyphase SRC tables, median/Hampel impulse suppression, envelope followers, crossover coefficients, saturator curves, LUFS maths, and dither. Keep `src/lib/audio/sonifier.ts` as the graph builder and live-control layer. Native Web Audio nodes are fine for simple filters, gain, panning, delay, and basic shaping, because `BiquadFilterNode`, `IIRFilterNode`, `WaveShaperNode`, `DynamicsCompressorNode`, `DelayNode`, `ChannelSplitterNode`, and `ChannelMergerNode` all exist already. The moment you need sample-accurate sidechains, multiband recombination, relative-threshold de-essing, or a true look-ahead limiter, you have crossed into `AudioWorklet` territory. `bridge/recorder.ts` should own the heavy path: offline resampling, optional AR de-clicking, LUFS/true-peak pass, and dither. citeturn17search0turn17search1turn17search2turn17search3turn18search0turn18search1turn18search5turn17search17turn0search11

The rollout order I would use is hard and simple. **P0, first shipping pass:** polyphase export SRC, conservative spike suppression, proper look-ahead limiting, loudness plus true peak, and TPDF dither. Those fix the “unacceptably bad” parts. **P1, second pass:** 3-band dynamics, relative-threshold de-esser or dynamic shelf, asymmetric saturation with oversampling, and mono-safe pseudo-stereo. Those give the big aesthetic lift. **P2, third pass:** soft expander plus comfort-noise bed, more adaptive control logic, and optional noise-shaped dither for listener exports. **P3, artistic pack:** tape wow/flutter, multiband waveshaping, wavefolder, Haas tricks, and animated filter drift. If you keep matched-bypass and latency-compensated comparisons for every stage, you will preserve the “raw earth” reference while still letting the production path sound like an intentional artwork instead of an accelerated sensor dump. citeturn38view1turn29view0turn35view0turn15view1turn40view0turn34view0turn20view0turn30view2
